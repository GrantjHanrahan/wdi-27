Career and contributions

Throughout this career, there was a common epistemological theme underlying von Neumann's works. As such, a significant portion of his time in mathematics was spent reformalizing axiomatic mathematics, namely introducing techniques to resolve earlier inconsistencies.

Once a strong axiomatic foundation had been built, his next consideration was whether such a system would be capable of providing definitive answers on questions posed to it.

Kurt GÃ¶del provided a proof, now known as the first incompleteness theorem, which stated this was not the case.

Building upon previous work, von Neumann also axiomatized quantum mechanics. By treating position or momentum as a linear operator, von Neumann showed that the evolution of a quantum system over time was isomorphically similar to a point in Hilbert space (generalization of 2-D vector space to n-dimensions).
In the late 1930s, von Neumann became recognized as the leading expert in the quantitative description of explosive phenomena. As a result, von Neumann was involved in the design of nuclear weapons.

In his model of an atomic bomb, von Neuamnn conceptualized he idea of explosive lensing, which internally reflected neutrons from U-238. This resulted in an implosion which brought the plutonium to critical mass for detonation.

Von Neumann was part of the committee responsible for choosing a detonation site for this atomic bomb. Many factors were taken into account, such as blast radius, death tolls, optimum shockwave propagation, and lasting atmospheric radiation.

His first choice was Kyoto. This decision was overruled, and the Fat Man atomic bomb was dropped on Nagasaki instead.
