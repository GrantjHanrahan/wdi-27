On December 28 of 1903, Neumann János Lajos, (a name equivlant in English to John Louis), was born in Budapest in the Austro-Hungarian empire.

He was the eldest of three brothers; his two younger siblings were Michael (b. 1907) and Nicholas (b. 1911).

John's father, Neumann Miksa, was a lawyer by education but banker by trade. John's mother was Kann Margit (English: Margaret Kann).

He was born a non-observant Jewish family, and was an agnostic throughout his entire life. However, on his deathbed, he seemed to admit that Pascal had a point and converted to Catholicism.

Von Neumann's academic life began early. He had a photographic memory, and by the age of 8, was fluent in English, French, German, Italian, and Ancient Greek.

At age 15, von Neumann studied under Gábor Szegő. Szegő was himself an accomplished mathematician, whose contributions in analysis had a significant impact in applied mathematics, theoretical physics and statistics. Szegő, astounded by von Neumann's genius, was brought to tears on their first day.

Despite von Neumann's influence in the mathematics and the sciences, he was not the most pleasant person to be associated with outside an academic setting.

He enjoyed being in loud environments, and while living on campus, disturbed his neighbours playing music continuously. He criticized his wife for preparing a study area that was too quiet, and began to work in the living room with the tv playing instead.

His poor driving ability resulted in many arrests and accidents. Nevertheless, he enjoyed driving while simultaneously reading a book.
